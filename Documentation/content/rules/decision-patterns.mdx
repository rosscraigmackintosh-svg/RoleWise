# Decision patterns
Decision patterns describe how Rolewise consistently interprets roles. They're rules for the system, not the user.
## Why this exists
Job descriptions vary widely. Without consistent interpretation, the same role might be read differently. Decision patterns ensure similar signals are interpreted similarly.
## What decision patterns are
Decision patterns are structured interpretations of common signals found in job descriptions.
They define how Rolewise:
- identifies meaningful signals
- groups related information
- interprets ambiguity
- surfaces potential risks or blockers
## Types of patterns
### Clarity vs ambiguity
Rolewise looks for how clearly a role is defined.
Signals of clarity:
- specific responsibilities
- defined scope
- clear expectations

Signals of ambiguity:
- broad or overlapping responsibilities
- unclear ownership
- vague success criteria

Ambiguity isn't negative, but it matters.

### Scope and ownership
Interpreted scope includes breadth, ownership, and autonomy. Large scope with low clarity signals friction.

### Expectation signals
High expectations appear as aggressive language, competing priorities, and unclear support. Decision patterns surface these.

### Missing information
What's unsaid matters. Rolewise highlights missing team structure, reporting lines, success metrics, and product maturity.

### Trade-offs
Roles involve trade-offs: ownership vs support, speed vs structure, impact vs clarity. Patterns make these visible.
## How patterns are used
```
Signals → Patterns → Interpretation → Output
```

Decision patterns are applied during:
- Applicant Mode (to interpret the role)
- Decision Snapshot (to structure what was seen)
- Weekly Review (to identify repeated signals)

They shape output without being shown as rules.
## What they are not
Decision patterns are not:
- rigid classifications
- scoring systems
- fixed templates
- predictions of success

They guide interpretation, not outcomes.
## Consistency over time
Same patterns across all roles enable consistent interpretation, meaningful comparison, and pattern recognition. The system becomes more useful as more roles are analysed.
## In one sentence
Decision patterns ensure roles are interpreted consistently, so patterns can be understood over time.
