# No Scoring Doctrine

> Rolewise will never produce a numeric score representing candidate fit, match percentage, compatibility rating, or any equivalent.

This is a constitutional rule. It is not a design preference or a current product decision. It governs all features, present and future.

## The argument

Scores feel precise but aren't. A "72% match" masks subjective weightings, incomplete data, and invisible assumptions. The number creates false confidence.

Scores also shift your relationship with the product. Instead of developing judgment, you defer to the number — and become less capable at evaluating roles over time.

Finally, scores are gameable. Users adjust behaviour to optimise the number, not their actual fit.

## What is permitted instead

Rolewise may surface:

- Structured breakdowns of role requirements
- Clear identification of gaps between requirements and background
- Evidence-based signals about patterns (e.g., repeated rejection at the same stage)
- Qualitative assessments with explicit reasoning visible to the user

The reasoning is visible and the judgment stays with you.

## Addressing objections

**"Users want scores — it's what they're used to."**

Familiarity doesn't equal utility.

**"Without scores, how do candidates compare roles?"**

By reading the structured analysis and applying judgment. Harder than scanning a number. More honest and durable.

**"Competitors offer scores."**

It's a reason to explain clearly why we don't.
